Theoretical Limits on Directionality of Sound
The primary factor that governs the directionality of sound is the diffraction limit, which is determined by the relationship between wavelength \lambda and the aperture size D. This relationship is important because sound cannot be arbitrarily focused without an appropriately large aperture relative to the wavelength.
Diffraction Limit
For any wave, including sound, the beamwidth \theta is limited by diffraction. The general formula for diffraction from a circular aperture is:
\theta = 1.22 \frac{\lambda}{D}
Where:
\theta is the angular width of the sound beam,


\lambda is the wavelength of the sound,


D is the diameter of the sound source or the aperture.


From this equation, the larger the aperture D, the smaller the beamwidth \theta for a given wavelength \lambda. However, for low-frequency sound, which is often used in long-range transmission due to its lower attenuation, \lambda becomes large, and the required aperture D grows dramatically.
Wavelength and Frequency Relationship
The wavelength \lambda is related to the frequency f by the equation:
\lambda = \frac{c}{f}
Where:
c is the speed of sound in air (approximately 343 m/s at room temperature),


f is the frequency of the sound.


For instance, at a frequency of 1 kHz, the wavelength is:
\lambda = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{1000 \text{ Hz}} = 0.343 \text{ meters}
At a frequency of 100 Hz, the wavelength is:
\lambda = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{100 \text{ Hz}} = 3.43 \text{ meters}
For low frequencies like 100 Hz, a significant aperture (multiple meters across) is needed to focus the sound into a narrow beam. This requirement makes the creation of highly directional low-frequency sound difficult without extremely large and impractical devices.

Air Absorption and Propagation Loss
As sound travels through the air, its intensity decreases due to two primary factors: geometric spreading and air absorption.
Geometric Spreading
The sound energy is spread over a larger area as it travels. The intensity of sound diminishes by the square of the distance, so the intensity I at a distance r is given by:
I \propto \frac{1}{r^2}
This means that as the distance from the source increases, the intensity of sound decreases significantly.
Air Absorption
Air absorbs sound energy, especially at higher frequencies. The attenuation A due to air absorption can be expressed as:
A = \alpha f^2
Where:
\alpha is a constant that depends on temperature, pressure, and humidity,


f is the frequency of the sound.


This means that higher frequencies experience stronger attenuation. For example, ultrasound (above 20 kHz) attenuates much faster than audible sound. Thus, for long-range transmission, low frequencies are preferred because they suffer less attenuation over large distances. However, as we saw earlier, this comes with the tradeoff of requiring large apertures for narrow-beam propagation.
